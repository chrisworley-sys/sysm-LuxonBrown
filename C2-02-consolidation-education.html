The Hidden Cost of Managing Money in Multiple Places

Hi [First Name],

Most people don't think of having accounts at multiple institutions as a problem. And in isolation, it isn't. But when you're building a retirement income plan, the fragmentation can quietly work against you in ways that are easy to miss.

Here are a few of the most common issues we see:

Overlapping or conflicting investment positions — When accounts aren't coordinated, you may be unknowingly duplicating risk or canceling out diversification benefits.

Tax inefficiency — Different account types (IRA, 401k, brokerage) have different tax treatments. Without a unified withdrawal strategy, you may be paying more in taxes than necessary.

Missed rebalancing — When your advisor can only see part of your portfolio, they can't rebalance the whole picture — only the slice they manage.

Complexity at retirement — Managing multiple accounts, statements, and logins becomes significantly more burdensome at exactly the time when simplicity matters most.

Consolidating doesn't mean giving up control — it means gaining clarity. And for many of our clients, it's one of the most impactful decisions they've made.
